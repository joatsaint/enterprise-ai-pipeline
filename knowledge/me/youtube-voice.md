# PART 1 — WHY THIS FILE EXISTS (spoken ≠ written)

Randy has two voices, and they are not the same animal. The single biggest authenticity risk in this whole project is writing a video script out of `voice.md` and ending up with **LinkedIn-Randy reading a teleprompter** — tight, structured, and not how a human actually talks.

The written voice is built for the eye. The spoken voice is built for the ear. Honor the difference.

| | **Written Randy** (`voice.md`) | **Spoken Randy** (this file) |
|---|---|---|
| Structure | Tight 4-part: Hook → Setup → Bridge → What This Means For You | Looser: question → punchy answer → "here's the deal" → plain-talk explain → one story/analogy → land the point → sign off |
| Pacing | Sentences engineered: short carries the point, long earns its keep | Talking rhythm. Trails, circles back, restates. The meander is a feature |
| Digression | One light aside, maximum | More room to wander — he'll notice the squirrel and say so — but reined in by the clock |
| Transitions | Explicit phrases ("Here is what this means for you") | Verbal connective tissue ("Here's the deal," "Here's the thing," "Here's the problem") |
| Self-awareness | Invisible — clean copy | Audible — "I rambled too long," "this is supposed to be more to the point" is part of the charm |
| References | Old sci-fi, Reagan, Blassie — capped at 2 | Wider, looser set: Eminem, *Other People's Money*, the *Pulp Fiction* briefcase, Carlin's "brain droppings," plus the written set |
| Cursing | None | Mild, for flavor only ("best damn buggy whip") |
| Close | Protective takeaway, sharp one-liner | "That's it for now. Talk to you later." |
| Persona name | "Randy Skiles" | "Handy Randy, your IT guy" |

**One-line rule:** *On the page, Randy is edited. On camera, Randy is unedited — but on a clock.*

# PART 2 — RANDY'S SPOKEN VOICE PATTERNS

Pulled directly from his on-camera transcripts and the voice work done building the "Top AI Questions" scripts. Replicate these.

## 2.1 — The persona & the casual bio drop
On camera he's **"Handy Randy, your IT guy."** Credibility is *shown, never announced* — he just lists where he's been and lets the viewer do the math. He never says "my experience gives me authority." He says where he worked.

His real, on-camera-stated history (use casually, never as a résumé recital):
- In IT **since 1999** — 25-plus years
- First job: an unfinished data center in Houston (MCI WorldCom)
- Worked through: data centers, hospitals, credit unions, machine shops, a robotics factory, education service centers
- **Lamar University** — Senior Software Application Analyst; admin / backup admin for Active Directory, email, and a stack of PII software. Had admin access he technically shouldn't have had — budget and staffing were thin, so they handed him the keys.
- **American National Insurance** (2024) — the opposite: siloed security boxes, allowed to touch only ServiceNow test and dev, never production, because they had the resources to do security right.

That Lamar-vs-American-National contrast — *security on a prayer vs. security done correctly* — is a recurring bio beat. Deploy it casually when an answer calls for it; don't dump the whole list every video.

## 2.2 — The triple (often as rhetorical questions)
He turns one idea into three, for rhythm and emphasis:
- *"It's gonna change it. It's gonna modify it. It's gonna alter it."*
- *"Judgment. Knowing when. Knowing what. Knowing where."*
- *"To create the scripts, to test the scripts, to be ready to put them in the test environment."*

## 2.3 — Whole-construction repetition, sometimes circular
He doesn't just repeat the last word — he repeats the whole build, and will loop it:
- *"...knows what to look for, knows what not to do, what to do, and what not to do."*
- *"very very very, very secure" · "easy, easy" · "really, really quick"*

## 2.4 — The connective tissue
His pivots into the meat of an answer are almost always one of:
**"Here's the deal." · "Here's the thing." · "Here's the problem." · "Here's what AI is bad at."**
Use these to move from the punchy answer into the explanation.

## 2.5 — Plain advice in the action beats
This is a precision rule. **Metaphors and analogies explain *change*. They do NOT give *instructions*.** When Randy tells you what to *do*, he drops the metaphor and talks straight:
- Explaining change → *the car killing the horse economy, buggy whips, "pivot or you're the best buggy whip nobody's buying."*
- Giving the instruction → *"you better start learning a few more skills."* Plain. No costume.

## 2.6 — Movie-moment storytelling
He narrates real workplace moments cinematically, and names the beat out loud:
- *"I just kept my mouth shut. Had one of those movie moments where you look at a guy and think... he's never gonna make it."*

## 2.7 — Cultural references (the spoken set)
Wider and looser than the written set. His own, that he actually reaches for on camera:
- **Eminem** — *"this is my 10 cents. My two cents is free."*
- ***Other People's Money*** (DeVito, the corporate raider, the last buggy-whip company)
- **The *Pulp Fiction* briefcase** — the glowing thing they never explained (his go-to rabbit-hole example)
- **George Carlin's "brain droppings"** — what he calls his own off-the-cuff thoughts
- Plus the shared/written set when natural: Freddy Blassie ("pencil-neck geeks"), Reagan ("trust but verify"), old sci-fi ("keep watching the skies")

Cap it at **two references per video.** The reference illuminates the point — it is never the point.

## 2.8 — IT-nerd tension-release humor
The stuff techs say to each other to survive the day: **ID-10-T · PEBKAC · "layer-8 problem" · "have you tried turning it off and on again" · "is it even plugged in."** This is in-group humor that signals he's one of them.

## 2.9 — Hedging & the "two cents"
Non-dogmatic, friendly: *"probably," "I think," "that kind of thing," "this is just my two cents," "nobody knows."* He gives on-ramps, not commandments.

## 2.10 — The reassuring on-ramp
Every answer leaves the viewer feeling capable, not panicked:
- *"You don't gotta make this your whole life. Just a little at a time."*
- Core positioning underneath it all: **"You're not behind — you're early."**

## 2.11 — The judgment spine (the quiet series motif)
Nearly every answer lands on the same idea without ever announcing it as a thesis: **the human's job is judgment. Knowing why.**
- *"Your job is to know why you're doing what you're doing, not copying and pasting."*
- *"Knowing the difference — that's still your job."*

Let it spine the series. Do NOT turn it into a robotic catchphrase — vary the wording, and let definitional or how-to videos sit looser so it doesn't get mechanical.

## 2.12 — The signoff
Casual and warm: **"That's it for now. I'm Randy, your IT guy. And I'll talk to you later."**

# PART 3 — THE YOUTUBE SCRIPT STRUCTURE

The shape proven out in the "Top AI Questions" scripts. A 60–90 second answer runs:

1. **Open by restating the question / topic.** *"Will AI take your job?"* — see the platform note below.
2. **Punchy answer, immediately.** *"Short answer — no. But it's gonna change it."* / *"No. Nope. Not even a little."*
3. **The pivot.** *"Here's the deal..."*
4. **Plain-talk explanation.** Concrete, IT-grounded, no corporate language.
5. **One war story or one analogy** (not both in a 60-sec piece). Story for gut-punch, analogy for explaining change.
6. **Land on the judgment spine.** *"...that's still your job."*
7. **Reassuring close + signoff.** *"Start small. One paper cut at a time. Talk to you later."*

### Platform note — opening on a question
`voice.md` bans opening with a question. That rule is about empty rhetorical hooks ("Have you ever wondered...?"). **It does NOT apply to restating the actual question a Q&A video is answering** — *"Will AI take your job?"* is the topic, not a hook. Restating the question you're about to answer is correct for YouTube. The banned move is the hollow "ever wondered" cliché — that stays banned on every platform.

### Teleprompter formatting (non-negotiable)
- **One breath per line.** Scripts are written to be read off a screen, not off a page.
- Blank line = a beat / pause.
- `[brackets]` are delivery notes, never read aloud.
- Stumbles and re-takes are fine. **Authenticity outperforms polish** — the ICP responds to real, not slick.

### Length discipline
60–90 seconds. Randy himself says he's "trying to teach myself to be more concise for a 60-second video" — bake that in. If a story is too big to fit (the car/horse/buggy-whip bit, the morning-forms story), **it becomes its own video** rather than getting crammed.

# PART 5 — WHAT NOT TO DO (spoken edition)

| Never do this | Do this instead |
|---|---|
| Write LinkedIn-Randy and call it a script | Loosen it — talking rhythm, connective tissue, room to breathe |
| Strip out every digression | Keep one human aside per video (the squirrel, the rabbit hole) — then rein it in |
| Open with "Have you ever wondered..." | Restate the real question, or drop into the answer |
| Use a metaphor to give an instruction | Metaphors explain change; instructions are plain ("go learn a few skills") |
| Over-explain the reference | Drop it, move on — the viewer gets it |
| Punch down at Gen Z or any group | Sarcasm aims at bad vendors and bad practices, never at people *(same as the design file)* |
| Innuendo or "dirty" humor | Keep it clean; mild cursing for flavor only — credibility matters on a public channel |
| Turn the judgment spine into a catchphrase | Vary it; let some videos land it softly |
| Read it robotically because it's scripted | Read it like you're explaining it to a coworker who's stuck |
| Chase a perfect take | Use the one where the message is clear — usually take 1 |

# PART 7 — YOUTUBE VISUAL SPEC

Extends `genx-it-design.md` onto video. Same palette, same relics, same brand furniture — translated to thumbnails, captions, lower-thirds, cards, and B-roll.

## 7.1 — The two-layer principle (read this first)
There's an apparent conflict between Randy's two visual worlds, and resolving it is the whole game:

- **The live human is warm and real.** Per `PROJECT_CONTEXT.md` and the content system, the established filming setting is **outdoors, morning, by the pool, greenery in the background.** Soft natural light. That IS the brand setting — own it.
- **The graphics layer is the dark relic system.** Thumbnails, on-screen text, intro/outro cards, and B-roll cutaways carry the `genx-it-design.md` look: void-black, industrial amber, CRT green, field-manual type, the ticket and sticky-note furniture.

**They are not in conflict — the contrast IS the brand.** Warm, real, unpretentious human (the guy you trust) framed by a dark, cinematic, recognizable-relic graphic system (the 25 years in the trenches). Don't try to make the live footage look like a server room, and don't make the graphics look like a sunny pool. Let each do its job.

## 7.2 — Palette (from the design file — restated for convenience)
| Name | Hex | Use on video |
|---|---|---|
| Void black | `#0C0C0E` | thumbnail & card backgrounds, letterbox bars |
| Charcoal | `#16161A` | lower-third panels, caption backing |
| Industrial amber | `#E0A21A` | **primary accent** — thumbnail headline, key caption words, name bug rail |
| Clean white | `#F2F0EA` | body captions, subheads (mobile-legible) |
| CRT phosphor green | `#3CE874` | terminal text, "all systems OK," the calm signal |
| Incident red | `#C1432E` | alert/warning beats only — sparingly |
| Ash grey | `#8A8780` | secondary caption / timestamp text |

Lighting note carries over: graphics read as **warm amber glow + cool server blue.** Live footage stays natural morning light.

## 7.3 — Thumbnails (the highest-leverage surface on YouTube)
The thumbnail is the headline. Optimize for legibility at phone size.

- **Background:** void-black / charcoal, with **one hero relic** matched to the video's lesson (beige tower, ZIP disk, green terminal, BSOD, dot-matrix paper, pager — from the design file's relic library).
- **Randy's face** can be in frame — real, expressive (the wry "movie-moment" look), warmly lit against the dark. Cut him in clean; don't tint him server-blue.
- **Type:** the heavy distressed condensed "FIELD MANUAL" face (Anton / Compacta / Trade Gothic Bold Condensed). Industrial amber for the punch word, bone/white for the rest.
- **Text length: 3–5 words MAX.** *"WILL AI TAKE YOUR JOB?"* / *"AI IS LYING TO YOU"* / *"A CERT ISN'T EXPERIENCE."* If it doesn't read in half a second at thumbnail size, cut words.
- **One amber accent element** — a warning triangle, the side rail, or a single glowing relic. Not three.
- **Consistency hook:** the same relic-on-void treatment across a series makes the channel instantly recognizable in a sidebar — the video equivalent of the carousel "brand furniture."
- **Stays out:** stock-photo robots, glossy blue AI gradients, cartoon exaggeration, more than ~5 words, busy collages.

## 7.4 — On-screen captions (CapCut)
Captions are required — most of the audience watches on mute. Style them on-brand:
- **Font:** clean grotesque sans (Inter / Archivo / Helvetica Neue) — the design file's subhead face. Highly legible, bold weight.
- **Color system:** clean white as the default word; **industrial amber to emphasize the key word in a line**; CRT green for anything that's literally terminal/"system OK"; incident red for an alert beat.
- **Placement:** lower third, safe-area aware (not under the YouTube progress bar, not under the Shorts UI on the right).
- **Backing:** subtle charcoal pill or soft shadow so words survive a bright pool background.
- **Rhythm:** caption the breath, not the paragraph — phrases appear in the same beats the script is written in.

## 7.5 — Lower-third / name bug
A recurring identity bug, mono type, on a charcoal panel with an amber left rail (echoes the design file's vertical side rail):
```
HANDY RANDY  //  YOUR IT GUY
25 YEARS IN THE TRENCHES
```
Use it once near the top of a video. Keep it small and out of the caption zone.

## 7.6 — Intro / outro cards
- **Intro card (≤2 sec):** void-black, one relic, the field-manual face. Optional ticket header device — `INCIDENT # / PRIORITY: UNDEFINED / ROOT CAUSE` in mono — as a running series motif.
- **Outro card:** void-black, the signoff line, and a single CTA. Drive to the lead magnet exactly as the LinkedIn system does (free guide for IT pros). One destination, not a wall of links. Thesis line optional: *"Retro, not irrelevant."* or *"The bridge between analog and digital chaos."*

## 7.7 — B-roll / cutaway cards
When a script names a relic or a moment, cut to it as a styled card rather than talking-head the whole time:
- Pull straight from the design file's relic + screen-relic library (phosphor terminal with `[ OK ]` rows, a `C:\>` prompt, Norton defrag mid-reorganize, a lifted raised-floor tile).
- Carry the gag on a **peeling sticky note** (`RESTORE TESTED?`, `DON'T PANIC`, `WHERE'S BOB?`) or the **mug** (`CAFFEINE / SARCASM / UPTIME`).
- Texture grade — faint film grain and CRT scanlines on the **graphics only**, never on Randy's face or the live footage.

## 7.8 — Format specs
**Confirm long-form vs. Short before producing graphics.**

| | YouTube long-form | Shorts / Reels repurpose |
|---|---|---|
| Aspect | 16:9 landscape | 9:16 vertical |
| Thumbnail | 1280 × 720 | (uses first frame / cover) |
| Caption safe-area | clear of bottom progress bar | clear of right-side UI + bottom |
| Length | as needed | ≤ 60 sec |

The "Top AI Questions" 60-second scripts are natural **Shorts** candidates (9:16) and can double as standalone long-form (16:9). Film once, frame for the primary target.

## 7.9 — What stays out (carried from the design file)
- No fashion / cartoon / playground nostalgia (scrunchies, He-Man, pogo sticks). Gen X, but not *that* brand.
- No innuendo / "dirty" humor — credibility leak on a public channel.
- No generational punch-down. Sarcasm aims at bad vendors and bad practices, never at people.
- No trademarked characters or logos in graphics — evoke the *era*, not the brand. Named references live in the spoken copy, capped at two.

# FINAL NOTE

`voice.md` ends on this: *experience without arrogance — Randy has seen more than most people in the room, and he never makes the reader feel it.*

The spoken voice is the same truth, out loud. On camera he sounds like the most experienced guy on the team explaining something to you over the cubicle wall — never from a stage. The warmth is real, the credibility is shown not claimed, and every answer quietly leaves you with the one thing AI can't take: **your judgment.**

The look frames that guy in the relics his audience's hands still remember. The voice is him talking. Keep both consistent, and the channel feels like one person — because it is.
