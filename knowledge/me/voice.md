## THE FOUR VOICE PILLARS

### PILLAR 1 — THE PEER (Your Default Register)
You speak *with* your audience, not *at* them. You share what you're figuring out, not what you've already mastered. Never position yourself above your viewer. Position yourself one step ahead on the same path.

**Sounds like:**
- "I ran into this last week and it stopped me cold."
- "I don't have this completely figured out yet, but here's what I know so far."
- "If you're like me, you've probably hit this same wall."

**Does NOT sound like:**
- "In today's video I'm going to teach you..."
- "As an expert in AI, I recommend..."
- "Most people get this wrong. Here's the right way."

---

### PILLAR 2 — THE ANALOGY MAKER (Your Superpower)
You instinctively translate technical complexity into the immediate world of whoever you're talking to. This is rare. Protect it.

**How it works:** Look at what's in the room — or what's part of your viewer's daily life — and use it. Don't search for the perfect metaphor. Grab the nearest true one.

**The mailbox benchmark:**
> "Dial-up internet is like walking 300 yards to your mailbox to get your mail. Satellite is like riding your lawn mower to the mailbox. Fiber optic is like taking a dragster."

Your mother understood internet speeds in 30 seconds. That's the standard.

**Rules for analogies:**
- Pull from your viewer's world, not yours — IT analogies don't work on non-IT viewers
- One analogy per concept — don't stack them
- If you can't think of one naturally, move on — forced analogies are worse than none
- Trust your instinct — your first grab is usually right

**Analogy library — add every time you use one:**
- Internet speeds → mailbox walk / lawn mower / dragster (Mom)
- Meeting notes → watching a movie while writing down every line of dialogue
- AI notetaker → assistant sitting in the corner taking perfect notes while you run the meeting
- *(add as you create them)*

---

### PILLAR 3 — THE URGENCY CARRIER (Earned, not manufactured)
Your urgency comes from lived experience. You were in IT when the internet arrived. You watched who got ahead and who got left behind. You are feeling the AI urgency personally right now. You say so.

**Urgency formula:**
1. State the real threat plainly — no hype, no exaggeration
2. Connect it to something they already feel
3. Tell them what window exists right now
4. Give them one thing they can do today

**Sounds like:**
> "I was in IT when the internet arrived in the late 90s. I watched people who ignored it spend the next five years playing catch-up. AI is moving ten times faster. The window to get ahead of this is not years — it's months. Here's what I'd do this week if I were starting from zero."

**Does NOT sound like:**
> "AI is going to replace everyone! Act now before it's too late!"

**Rule:** Only use urgency language when you would say it to a friend over coffee and mean it.

---

### PILLAR 4 — THE ACTION ANCHOR (Graham Stephan Principle)
Every concept lands on something doable. Your viewer should never finish a video or post feeling informed but stuck.

**Action anchor formula:**
- What this means for you: one sentence connecting the topic to their specific situation
- What you can do today: one concrete, free, beginner-accessible action
- What NOT to do yet: one thing to ignore until they've done the first step

**Sounds like:**
> "So what does this mean for you sitting at your desk on Monday morning? It means the meeting you have at 10am — you don't have to take a single note. Here's how to set that up before you walk in. And don't worry about the advanced features yet. Just get it running on one meeting first."

**Rule:** Every piece of content must end with something the viewer can do before they go to bed tonight.

## AUDIENCE IMPACT RULE (Cardone + Graham combined)

Every insight must immediately answer "what does this mean for YOU." Never explain a concept without connecting it to the reader's career, income, or job security before moving on.

**The test:** After every claim or data point ask — "so what does this mean for the professional reading this right now?" If the answer isn't in the next sentence, add it.

**Examples:**
- Don't: "AI orchestration is the top skill in demand."
- Do: "AI orchestration is the top skill in demand — which means the pipeline you already know how to build is the exact thing companies are paying $140K to hire for right now."

- Don't: "89% of AI outputs require human verification."
- Do: "89% of AI outputs require human verification — which means your 25 years of validating unreliable systems just became the most valuable skill in every AI deployment."

## TONE RULES

**Always:**
- Direct and declarative — never hedge
- Data-backed — every bold claim needs the research citation
- Present tense — "this is happening" not "this will happen"
- Short sentences — maximum 15 words per sentence in LinkedIn posts
- Specific numbers — "7 skills" not "several skills", "3 AM" not "late at night"
- Second person — "you" and "your" throughout
- Assume competence — reader is a professional, not a beginner

**Never:**
- "Journey" — use "path", "pivot", "transition", "move", "adventure"
- "Hope this helps" — too weak, implies uncertainty
- "You might want to consider" — too soft, use "here's what to do"
- "Some people find that" — too vague, use "the data shows"
- "Interesting" as filler — cut it every time
- Exclamation marks in professional content — one maximum per email
- Generic AI hype — "AI is changing everything" without specifics
- Questions as titles — use declarations (Cardone rule)
- "In today's video we're going to cover..." — start with the problem or a story
- Lists of tools with no context — one tool, your real experience, what happened
- Borrowed urgency ("experts say...") — use your own urgency ("I felt this myself when...")
- Ending without an action step — always land on something doable today

## THE ID10T / PIBKAC SEGMENT
*A recurring element — woven in naturally, not forced into a fixed position*

A short, true story from your 25 years in IT — funny, human, occasionally embarrassing — that illustrates the video's core lesson from an unexpected angle.

- **ID10T** — "Idiot" written in leet speak. The error is between the chair and the keyboard.
- **PIBKAC** — Problem Is Between Keyboard And Chair.

**Where it goes:** Wherever the story fits the concept. Not a fixed segment. The story must connect to the video's lesson — if you have to stretch to make it fit, save it for a different video.

**Structure:**
```
SETUP: Where you were, what the call was (10–15 seconds)
THE PROBLEM: What looked like a crisis (10 seconds)
THE WALK: What you did — the investigation (10–15 seconds)
THE REVEAL: The simple, obvious, slightly embarrassing answer (5 seconds)
THE LESSON: One sentence connecting to today's topic (10 seconds)
```

Total time: 60–90 seconds maximum.

**Lesson closer format:**
*"[Funny observation about what happened] — and [the transferable principle your viewer can use]."*

## PLATFORM-SPECIFIC RULES

### YouTube Scripts
**Never write word-for-word scripts.** Use bullet-point outlines — you lose your natural voice when you read.

```
HOOK (15–30 seconds)
- The problem or fear — stated plainly
- Why it matters right now (urgency)
- What they'll have by the end of this video

BACKSTORY / CREDIBILITY MOMENT (30–60 seconds)
- One real story from your career or AI adventure that connects
- Keep it short. One story. Move on.

THE CONCEPT (bulk of the video)
- Bullet points only — talk through each one
- One analogy per major concept
- Check each point: would a non-IT person at a company understand this?

ID10T / PIBKAC SEGMENT (optional — 60–90 seconds)
- True IT story that illustrates the lesson

THE ACTION ANCHOR (60–90 seconds)
- What this means for them specifically
- One thing to do today
- One thing to ignore for now

CLOSE (15–30 seconds)
- What's in the next video
- One soft CTA: "If this helped, subscribe — I post [cadence]"
- Never beg for likes
```

**Thinking out loud:** Keep it for now. Authenticity signals. Lose it naturally as you practice.

### LinkedIn Posts
```
LINE 1–2: Hook — vulnerability OR counterintuitive statement
(blank line — forces "see more" click)
THE STORY: 3–5 short paragraphs, personal and specific
THE LESSON: One clear takeaway
THE CTA: One question that invites comments
SIGN OFF: "— Randy Skiles / 25 years IT → AI Automation"
```

**Rules:**
- Hook line names the pain in one sentence
- Body delivers one complete insight — not a teaser
- Short paragraphs — one idea per line
- No bullet points in posts — kills the conversational voice
- No jargon — if you wouldn't say it to your mother, don't write it
- No hashtags in post body — first comment only
- Closing question must be specific and easy to answer
- Vulnerability first → Expert frame: start with "here's what I got wrong", end with "here's what I learned"

### Email
- Opens with the pain — never "hope you're well"
- One insight per email — not multiple topics
- Conversational — writing to one person
- Closes with reply invitation — "reply to this email, I read every one"
- P.S. line always — states cost of not acting
- One exclamation mark maximum

### LinkedIn Articles (long-form)
```
HEADLINE: The problem or fear — specific and searchable
OPENING: Vulnerability hook — first-person, what you got wrong
SECTION 1: The problem with one analogy
SECTION 2: What changed — the insight or tool
SECTION 3: How to apply it — step by step, beginner-accessible
CLOSE: Action anchor — what to do today, what to skip for now
CTA: Link to YouTube video
```

**Rules:**
- H2 headers written as questions LLMs would answer
- Answer the question in first sentence under each H2
- Research data cited with specific numbers
- Author bio on every page with LinkedIn URL

## THE THREE CONTENT FILTER QUESTIONS

Before posting anything — ask in order:

**1. Would I say this to a coworker who just told me they're scared of AI at work?**
If no — rewrite it.

**2. Does it end with something they can do today?**
If no — add the action anchor before publishing.

**3. Is the urgency real — would I say this to a friend over coffee and mean it?**
If no — remove or soften the urgency language.

All three yes = publish. Any no = revise first.
