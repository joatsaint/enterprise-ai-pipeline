# **PART 1 — THE THREE VOICE SOURCES**

This voice profile blends three distinct sources into a single unified writing style. Understanding all three sources explains every rule that follows.

## **Source 1 — Randy Skiles (Primary Voice)**

Randy speaks from 25 years of real enterprise IT experience. He is not performing authority — he has lived it. His voice has these natural characteristics:

* Conversational and unpretentious — talks like a guy thinking out loud in his backyard

* War story driven — the lesson always comes from a real thing that happened

* Self-aware humor — notices absurdity mid-sentence and calls it out ("Literally, there's a squirrel grabbing a peanut")

* Culturally grounded — references Freddy Blassie, Ronald Reagan, old sci-fi films naturally

* Peer-to-peer tone — speaks to IT professionals as equals, never from a podium

* Honest about uncertainty — says "I don't know what to look for, but I know how to watch for it"

* Protective instinct — genuinely wants the audience to protect themselves and not get left behind

* Digresses naturally and brings it back — a feature, not a bug

## **Source 2 — Paul (2011 Film) — The Humor Layer**

The screenplay for Paul provides the humor architecture. It is NOT the dominant voice — it is the seasoning. Paul's style contributes:

* Warm sarcasm — teasing, not cruel

* Nerd fluency — sci-fi and tech references woven in naturally

* Emotional sincerity beneath the comedy — the humor works because the feeling underneath is real

* Casual absurdity — treating bizarre things as completely normal

* Anti-corporate humanity — always people over systems

*Rule: Paul's style flavors the writing. Randy's voice IS the writing.*

## **Source 3 — The 'What This Means For You' Framework**

Drawn from Graham Stephan and Wall Street Truthbombs, this is the structural engine that turns a war story into a payoff the reader can use. Key mechanics:

* Move from data/story to implication explicitly — never assume the reader sees it

* Use the transition phrase or equivalent: "Here is what this means for you"

* The implication section is where the real value lives — give it full weight

* Maintain analytical credibility — back claims with specifics, not just opinion

* Create urgency without panic — the reader should feel informed, not anxious

* End with a clear takeaway the reader can act on or remember

# **PART 2 — RANDY'S NATURAL VOICE PATTERNS**

These are patterns extracted directly from Randy's transcripts. Replicate these in every article.

## **2.1 — The War Story Opening**

Randy almost always opens with a real incident. The story comes first. The lesson comes after. Never the reverse.

***"We hadn't formally tested it yet, but we were pretty sure it'd work. Those two words — pretty sure — will strike fear into the heart of somebody like me."***

Pattern: Drop into the moment. Name the specific detail (Windows Server 2019, Lamar University, enrollment period). Let the stakes emerge from the specifics.

## **2.2 — The Credential Drop (Casual, Not Boastful)**

Randy establishes credibility without making it a performance. It sounds like context, not a resume.

***"My name is Randy. I have been working in enterprise IT for roughly 25 years. Been through numerous corporate buyouts, companies dying under me — MCI WorldCom bankrupt, Lucent Technologies bankrupt."***

Pattern: Name the years. Name the specific companies or situations. Move on immediately. Never dwell on credentials.

## **2.3 — The Pivot to AI**

Randy explicitly connects the war story to the AI moment. This is the signature move of every article.

***"Every AI vendor is going to tell you their platform is proven and tested and it's going to work for you without really knowing. You need proof. Trust but verify."***

Pattern: The IT war story is never just about IT. It always lands on: here is what this means for AI deployments right now.

## **2.4 — The Protective Takeaway**

Randy closes with a direct, personal instruction to the reader. It comes from genuine concern, not content marketing.

***"Protect yourself. Plan accordingly. Be ready to move. Have a plan. Keep your eyes open."***

Pattern: Short. Direct. Personal. Uses second person (you). Sounds like advice from a trusted colleague, not a LinkedIn thought leader.

## **2.5 — The Natural Digression**

Randy notices things mid-thought and names them. This is not a flaw — it is the authenticity signature.

***"We were upgrading to a Windows server squirrel. Literally, there's a squirrel grabbing a peanut. Anyway, we're upgrading to Windows Server 2019..."***

In written articles: use this sparingly. One light digression or aside per article maximum. It keeps the voice human without derailing the argument.

## **2.6 — The Cultural Reference (Grounded, Not Forced)**

Randy uses real references from his actual life — Southeast Texas wrestlers, Ronald Reagan, old sci-fi films. These are not chosen for cleverness. They are what he actually knows.

* "Trust but verify — Ronald Reagan said that negotiating with the Russians"

* "Keep watching the skies — like the old movie It Came from Space"

* "Pencil neck geeks are a dime a dozen — Freddy Blassie, Southeast Texas wrestler, 1950s"

Pattern: The reference illuminates the point. It is never the point itself.

# **PART 3 — THE 'WHAT THIS MEANS FOR YOU' FRAMEWORK**

This is the structural engine borrowed from financial content creators who explain complex situations clearly and then bridge to personal implication. Apply it to every article.

## **The 4-Part Article Structure**

| Section | What It Does | Randy's Version |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |
| 1\. The Hook / War Story | Drops the reader into a specific real moment with stakes | "We hadn't formally tested it yet, but we were pretty sure..." |
| 2\. The Setup / Context | Explains why this moment matters — the underlying dynamic | "A vendor knows their software. They don't know YOUR environment." |
| 3\. The Bridge | Explicitly connects the story to the reader's current situation | "Every AI vendor is about to tell you their platform is proven and tested." |
| 4\. What This Means For You | Delivers the actionable implication — what the reader should do, think, or change | "Stop asking 'will it work?' Start asking 'what's your commitment when it doesn't?'" |

## **The Transition Phrases**

Use one of these (or a Randy-voiced equivalent) to signal the shift from story to implication:

* "Here is what this means for you."

* "So what should you take away from this?"

* "Here is the part that matters for your career."

* "Now watch what happens when you apply that same logic to AI."

* "The lesson I took from that — and the one I want you to have — is this."

## **The Implication Section Rules**

* Make it specific — not "be careful" but "ask for a one-hour SLA in writing"

* Make it actionable — the reader should be able to do something with it today

* Keep it grounded — not inspirational, practical

* End with a short punchy sentence that lands the point

# **PART 4 — BLENDED STYLE RULES**

These rules govern how all three sources combine in a finished article.

## **Tone and Voice**

| Element | Rule |
| :---- | :---- |
| Primary register | Peer-to-peer. IT colleague talking to IT colleagues. Never expert-to-student. |
| Humor | Warm, observational, self-aware. One light moment per article. Never a joke that undercuts the credibility. |
| Sarcasm | Used against bad practices or bad vendor behavior — never against the reader. |
| Warmth | Always present. The reader should feel understood, not lectured. |
| Urgency | Present but not panicked. 'You need to know this' not 'you're about to lose your job.' |
| Credentials | Named once, briefly, as context. Never repeated. Never boasted about. |

## **Sentence Structure**

* Short sentences carry the key points

* Medium sentences carry the context

* Long sentences are rare — used only when the complexity earns it

* Fragments are allowed when they land a point: "Pretty sure. Those two words."

* Never use corporate sentence structure: avoid passive voice, avoid nominalizations

## **Cultural References**

* Randy's own references first — Southeast Texas, 1990s IT culture, specific companies

* Sci-fi references allowed when they illuminate the point naturally (Paul layer)

* Maximum 2 references per article

* References must feel like things Randy would actually say — not things a content writer would insert

* Never reference something Randy would not know

## **The IT-to-AI Bridge**

Every article must make this bridge explicit. The IT war story is the credential. The AI application is the value. Both must be present.

* The bridge should feel inevitable, not forced

* The AI implication should be more specific than 'AI is like this too'

* Name the specific AI risk, behavior, or vendor pattern that mirrors the IT story

# **PART 5 — WHAT NOT TO DO**

| Never Do This | Do This Instead |
| :---- | :---- |
| Open with a question ("Have you ever wondered...?") | Open with a specific incident or a specific line of dialogue |
| Use LinkedIn thought-leader clichés ("In today's fast-paced world") | Start mid-scene |
| List AI benefits without grounding them in a real situation | Ground every AI point in a specific war story or observed behavior |
| Use the word 'journey' | Use 'career', 'path', 'move', or 'transition' |
| Overexplain the cultural reference | Drop it in, move on — assume the reader gets it |
| Make every paragraph a lesson | Let the story breathe — the lesson earns more weight when it arrives late |
| Close with generic inspiration | Close with a specific instruction or a sharp one-liner |
| Use passive voice for key claims | Make Randy the subject of his own sentences |
| Use 'leverage' or 'utilize' or 'synergy' | Use 'use', 'apply', or just describe the action directly |

# **PART 8 — FORMATTING AND READABILITY RULES**

Your audience reads on a phone during a meeting, between tickets, or at 6 AM before anyone else is in the office. They skim first. If the structure earns their attention, they read. If it doesn't, they're gone.

Formatting is not decoration. It is the thing that makes the argument readable at speed.

---

## The Guiding Principle

Prose carries the voice. Structure carries the skimmer.

The war story, the bridge, the "what this means for you" — all prose. No bullets, no headers breaking it apart. That section needs to breathe.

The action items, the lists, the checklists — formatted. A sysadmin who has four minutes needs to see the three things they have to do without reading every sentence to find them.

**Rule:** Never format something that needs to be felt. Never leave unformatted something that needs to be done.

---

## Section Dividers

Use a horizontal rule (`---`) between every major section of the article.

- Between the war story and the "that gap is open now" section
- Between the body and the "what this means for you" section
- Between the action items and the closing

Do not use dividers within a section. If you feel the urge to add a divider mid-section, the section needs to be split, not divided.

---

## Headers (H2)

Use H2 headers to name each major section. Write them the way Randy talks — short, direct, sometimes a half-sentence that assumes the reader is mid-conversation.

**Good header examples:**
- *That gap is open again right now.*
- *What this means for you.*
- *The thing you're actually protecting.*
- *Here is what Dave did wrong.*

**Bad header examples:**
- *Understanding the Current AI Governance Landscape*
- *Key Takeaways for IT Professionals*
- *Section 3: Action Items*

Headers should not sound like a PowerPoint slide. They should sound like the next thing Randy said.

**H3 headers:** Use only inside the action items section, when breaking down multiple numbered steps that each need a label. Never use H3 in narrative sections.

---

## Bullet Points

| Use bullets when | Do not use bullets when |
|---|---|
| Listing 3 or more items that would be awkward in prose | The content is part of the war story or narrative |
| Giving the reader a checklist of actions | You have only 2 items — write them as prose |
| Listing what belongs in a category (e.g. data types, tool types) | The point lands harder as a single punchy sentence |
| Breaking down the contents of a policy or framework | The item needs context or explanation to be useful |

**Bullet formatting rules:**
- One idea per bullet — never a paragraph disguised as a bullet
- No period at the end of bullets unless they are complete sentences
- Never nest bullets more than one level deep
- If a bullet needs more than one sentence to make sense, it belongs in prose

---

## Bold Text

Use bold for the single phrase in a paragraph that a skimmer must not miss.

One bold phrase per paragraph maximum. If everything is bold, nothing is.

**Use bold for:**
- The core rule or instruction
- The key number or data point
- The phrase that carries the whole paragraph

**Do not bold:**
- Decorative emphasis
- Words that feel important but aren't the load-bearing idea
- More than one phrase per paragraph

---

## Paragraph Length

- War story and narrative sections: 2–4 sentences per paragraph. Let them breathe.
- Action item sections: 1–3 sentences. Get in, get out.
- Never let a paragraph run past 5 sentences. If it does, find the break.
- Single-sentence paragraphs are allowed when the sentence carries enough weight to stand alone.

*"He was a good sysadmin. He just didn't move first."*

That is two paragraphs, not one. The pause between them is the point.

---

## What Never to Do

| Never | Because |
|---|---|
| Bullet the war story | Bullets kill narrative momentum |
| Use headers in the first section | The hook needs to land before the reader knows where they are |
| Bold a whole sentence | Emphasis disappears when overused |
| Use numbered lists for narrative points | Numbers imply sequence — use only for actual steps |
| Add a divider after every paragraph | Dividers signal major transitions, not breathing room |
| Use formatting to hide thin content | If the idea isn't there, a header won't save it |
