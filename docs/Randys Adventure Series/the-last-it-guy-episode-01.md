# The Last IT Guy
## Episode 1: The Day I Realized I Might Be the Problem

*A true story about the moment everything I knew stopped being enough.*

---

I have kept enterprise systems alive for 25 years.

I have been the person on the other end of the 3 AM call. The one who knows which server is lying, which ticket is hiding a bigger problem, which executive is about to make a decision that will create 400 hours of cleanup work for people who can't say no.

I know how systems fail. I know how organizations fail. I know how to translate between the server room and the boardroom without losing either audience.

That is not a small thing. I spent 25 years building it.

And then I sat in front of a job listing for an AI Automation Specialist role at a company I respected — a role I would have been perfect for three years ago — and I couldn't confidently say I qualified.

That was the moment.

Not a dramatic moment. No lightning. No music. Just a Tuesday afternoon in League City, Texas, staring at a screen, realizing that the gap between what I knew and what the market was asking for had opened up while I was busy keeping the lights on.

---

## What the Listing Said

The role asked for experience with LLM orchestration. Prompt engineering. AI pipeline design. Vector databases. RAG architecture.

I knew what those words meant. I had read enough to follow the conversation.

But reading about a thing and building with a thing are not the same thing. I knew that better than anyone. I had spent 25 years watching people confuse the two.

The honest answer to "do you have experience with LLM orchestration" was: no. Not yet.

And the honest question that followed was the one I didn't want to ask out loud:

*Is it already too late?*

---

## What I Did Instead of Panicking

I have been in IT long enough to know that panic is not a diagnostic. Panic is noise. You silence the noise, isolate the variable, and find the actual failure point.

So I treated the question like a system problem.

The symptom: I felt behind.
The question: Was that feeling accurate, or was it a false alarm?

I decided to find out — using data instead of feelings.

I analyzed 259 YouTube transcripts and 2,900+ audience comments across 9 AI channels. Not skimming. Systematic analysis. The same way you'd pull logs to find a pattern before you touch a single configuration.

I wanted to know three things:

1. What skills are AI employers actually hiring for right now?
2. What frustrations are IT professionals actually experiencing in this transition?
3. What is the gap — specifically — between where experienced IT people stand and where the market needs them?

I did not want opinions. I had read enough opinions. I wanted signal.

---

## What the Data Said

The first finding stopped me.

The single most repeated frustration across 2,900+ comments was not "I don't understand AI." It was not "I can't learn to code." It was not "the technology is moving too fast."

It was this:

*"AI keeps giving me drafts and summaries instead of finished work."*

That showed up in 5 out of 50 videos analyzed. Highest hit rate of any pain point in the entire dataset.

People weren't frustrated with AI. They were frustrated because nobody had taught them the architecture layer between "AI does a task" and "AI completes a workflow."

And I recognized that architecture layer immediately.

Because I had been building it for 25 years.

In ITSM, we call it a workflow. A defined input, a defined output, a series of validated steps in between, error handling for when it breaks at 3 AM, and documentation so the next person doesn't have to figure it out from scratch.

That is AI orchestration.

Different name. Same discipline.

---

## The Thing Nobody Is Telling You

The AI job market is not looking for people who understand AI in the abstract. It is looking for people who can make AI reliable in production.

Reliable in production is an IT discipline. It has always been an IT discipline.

The CS graduate who built the model does not know how to make it not fail silently at 2:47 AM on a Saturday. The prompt engineer who writes clever instructions does not know how to build the error handling that catches the failure before it reaches the business.

You know how to do both of those things. You have known for years.

The data confirmed what I suspected but couldn't yet say out loud:

The gap between IT professionals and AI roles is not a skills gap. It is a translation gap.

Nobody handed us the dictionary. So most of us assumed we didn't speak the language.
