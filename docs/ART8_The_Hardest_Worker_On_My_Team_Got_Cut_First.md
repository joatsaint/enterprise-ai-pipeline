# The Hardest Worker On My Team Got Cut First

A guy I worked with spent eighteen months as the hardest-working person on our team. Tickets closed faster than anyone's. First one logged in, last one logged off. Management used him as the example in every all-hands: "This is what going above and beyond looks like."

Four months later he was gone. Same round of cuts that took two people who barely showed up.

I've been laid off twice in twenty-five years of enterprise IT — MCI WorldCom, Lucent Technologies, companies that felt too big to fail until they weren't. Both times I told myself the same thing on the drive home: I worked harder than half the people who kept their jobs. How does that happen?

It took me years to understand the real answer, and I think most people in IT right now are about to learn it the hard way, faster than I did, because AI is about to make the gap impossible to hide.

**Here's what I finally figured out: "hardworking" and "valuable" are not the same measurement, and most companies have never had a good way to tell them apart — until now.**

## The Old Way Companies Measured You

For most of my career, the honest truth is that nobody could measure your output very precisely. Management saw activity. Tickets closed. Hours logged. Projects shipped. Whether that activity actually moved the business forward — whether it reduced risk, saved money, kept a customer, prevented an outage nobody ever heard about — that was mostly a guess, filtered through whoever happened to notice you that quarter.

So "busy" became the proxy for "valuable." It was the best measurement anybody had. And if you were the person who looked the busiest, you generally felt safe.

That proxy is about to disappear. Not because anyone's being malicious about it — because AI just made it possible to measure the real thing directly.

## What's Actually Happening Right Now

I've been watching the layoff announcements out of companies like Cloudflare and Meta the same way I used to watch storm tracks before a hurricane hit the Gulf Coast — not for the headline, for the pattern underneath it.

And the pattern is consistent. It moves in stages. First, the roles built entirely around measuring and reporting on the business get trimmed — because AI can now do that measurement faster, more continuously, and with fewer blind spots than a team of people ever could. Then it moves to the roles that exist to move information between departments — the coordinators, the people who "know where everything lives." Then — and this is the part that should get every builder's attention — it reaches the people who actually produce things.

That last stage is the trap, and it's the one nobody sees coming until they're standing in it.

## The Trap Hiding Inside "10x Productivity"

Right now, plenty of engineers and analysts are having their best quarter ever. AI tools have made them genuinely faster. Leadership notices. Output goes up. Everybody feels good about it.

Then the company finishes building a better way to measure what that output is actually worth — and discovers that a lot of "more output" was just more activity. More tickets that didn't move the needle. More features nobody used. More reports that informed no decision anybody can name.

The person who was the hero of last quarter's productivity numbers becomes this quarter's hard question in a budget meeting. Not because they slowed down. Because the company finally built a ruler that measures something other than speed.

## What This Means for You

If your case for staying employed is "look how much I produce," you're standing on the same ground that's about to shift under a lot of good, hardworking people over the next two years. That's not a guess — it's the same sequence I've watched play out more than once across twenty-five years, just moving faster this time because AI is doing the measuring.

So here's what I'd tell that guy from my old team if I could go back and have one conversation with him before the cuts:

**Stop trying to prove you're busy. Start being able to answer one question in plain language: "What gets worse for this company if I'm not here?"**

Not "I closed 40 tickets this week." Try: "I caught the access misconfiguration that would've shown up in next quarter's audit." Not "I shipped the feature on time." Try: "I'm the reason the rollout didn't take down the system the way the last one did."

That's the difference between an artifact and an outcome. One of them shows up on a productivity dashboard. The other one is the reason you're still in the room when the headcount conversation happens.

**Practical moves, starting this week:**

- Take your last three "wins" and rewrite each one as: *here's what would have gone wrong without me.* If you can't fill in that blank, that's the work to walk away from first.
- Ask your manager directly: "What outcome are you actually being judged on this quarter — and how does my work move that number?" Most people have never asked this. The ones who have are the ones who keep getting funded.
- Pick one thing you do that's pure activity — a report, a status update, a recurring task — and use AI to compress it down to the smallest amount of your time possible. Then spend the time you got back on the part of your job that a dashboard can't measure: judgment, context, the call you make at 2 a.m. that keeps something from becoming a headline.

I'm not telling you this to scare you. I'm telling you because I've sat in that parking lot twice, replaying the work I did, wondering how it didn't add up to enough — and I'd rather you skip that part entirely.
