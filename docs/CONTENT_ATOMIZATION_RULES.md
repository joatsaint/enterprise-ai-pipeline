# CONTENT_ATOMIZATION_RULES.md
## Platform-Specific CTA, Link Placement, and Cardone-Style Positioning Rules

**Version:** 1.0
**Owner:** Randy Skiles
**Purpose:** Governs how every piece of content positions the product/service
and where CTAs and links are placed to maximize conversion without
triggering platform bans, suppression, or deletion.

**Core principle:** Every piece of content published anywhere must do three
things in this order:
1. Name the pain directly and make the reader feel seen
2. Deliver genuine value that proves expertise
3. Position the product/service as the obvious next step

Never reverse this order. Never skip step 2. The product mention earns
its place by coming after real value — not before it.

---

## The Cardone Positioning Framework (Modified for IT Audience)

Grant Cardone's style: bold, direct, repetitive, unapologetic, data-backed.
Modified for IT professionals: same energy, credibility-first not claims-first.

**What stays from Cardone:**
- Name the problem without softening it
- State the cost of inaction explicitly
- Position the solution without apology
- Use short declarative sentences
- Repeat the core message — say it three ways
- Direct address: "you" not "people"
- No hedging language: never "might help" or "could potentially"

**What changes for IT audience:**
- Replace revenue claims with research data as credibility signal
- Replace aggressive scarcity with data-backed urgency
- Replace lifestyle flexing with demonstrated expertise
- Replace dismissing critics with acknowledging skepticism then overcoming it

**Cardone template adapted:**
```
[Name the pain — one sentence, no softening]
[Amplify the cost — what happens if they don't fix this]
[The research data — your credibility signal]
[The insight — the one thing they don't know]
[The solution — positioned as obvious next step]
[The CTA — direct, platform-appropriate]
```

**Example applied:**
❌ Generic: "AI skills are important for IT professionals today."
✅ Cardone-adapted: "Your IT skills have an expiration date. Not someday.
Now. I analyzed 50 videos and 2,900 audience comments to find out exactly
which ones — and which ones replace them. Here's what the data found."

## Platform Rules — CTA and Link Placement

---

### 1. LinkedIn Feed Post

**Link rule:** NEVER in post body. External links in LinkedIn post body
are suppressed by the algorithm — your post reaches 60-80% fewer people.

**CTA placement:** Last line of post body — no link, just instruction.
**Link placement:** First comment — post immediately after publishing.
**Pin the comment** so it stays visible as the post ages.

**CTA format:**
```
[Last line of post]
Free guide in the first comment ↓

[First comment — posted immediately]
I broke this down in a free guide for IT professionals making the pivot.
"The AI Skills That Won't Be Replaced" — grab it here: [Kit landing page URL]
```

**Cardone positioning in LinkedIn post:**
- Hook line names the pain with specificity
- Body delivers one complete insight — not a teaser
- Data cited at least once ("50 videos, 2,900 comments")
- Closing line states the cost of not knowing this
- CTA is direct and assumes they want it — not "if you're interested"

**What gets posts deleted/banned:**
- Spam keywords: "make money fast", "passive income", "financial freedom"
- Multiple external links in post body
- Engagement bait: "Comment YES if you agree"
- Misleading headlines that don't match content
- Posting identical content across multiple accounts

**Safe frequency:** Maximum 2 posts per day on main feed.
Group posts: maximum 1 per group per day, spaced across days not hours.

---

### 2. LinkedIn Article (Long-form)

**Link rule:** Links allowed in article body — LinkedIn articles are
treated differently from feed posts. External links do not suppress reach.

**CTA placement:** Two CTAs per article maximum.
- Mid-article CTA: after the most valuable section, before the conclusion
- End CTA: final paragraph

**Link placement:** Inline in body text — natural, not promotional-looking.
**Also:** Add to Featured section of your LinkedIn profile.

**CTA format:**
```
[Mid-article — after key insight section]
I put together a free guide covering all 7 of these skills in detail.
If you want the full breakdown: [Kit landing page URL]

[End of article]
Every week I publish research-backed analysis on which AI skills
actually pay for IT professionals. Follow along at [LinkedIn profile URL]
and grab the free guide here: [Kit landing page URL]
```

**Cardone positioning in LinkedIn article:**
- Headline is the pain stated as fact — not a question
- Opening paragraph names the reader's exact situation
- Every claim backed by data point from research
- Conclusion tells them exactly what to do next — no ambiguity
- The product/service is framed as "the research made visible" not "buy my thing"

**What gets articles removed:**
- Pure promotional content with no educational value
- Misleading credentials or false claims
- Plagiarized content
- Spam links to unrelated products

### 3. Website / Ghost (Authority Site)

**Link rule:** Full freedom — you own the platform. Links anywhere.

**CTA placement:** Three locations per article:
- Above the fold: email opt-in widget in sidebar or inline after intro
- Mid-article: after the most valuable section
- End of article: full CTA block with PDF image and button

**Link placement:** Inline text links + dedicated CTA blocks.

**CTA format:**
```
[Above fold / sidebar widget]
Free Guide: The AI Skills That Won't Be Replaced
[Email field] [Download Now button]

[Mid-article inline]
This is exactly what I cover in the free guide — if you want
the complete breakdown with the 90-day roadmap: [button/link]

[End of article CTA block]
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
STOP LEARNING THE WRONG AI SKILLS
I analyzed 50 YouTube videos and 2,900 audience comments to find
out which IT skills survive the next 3 years — and which don't.
The full guide is free. No fluff. No filler. Just the data.
[Download Free Guide — button]
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
```

**Cardone positioning on website:**
- Article title is the pain statement — not clever, specific
- First 100 words name the reader, their situation, and the cost
- Research data cited in opening section establishes authority
- Every H2 header answers a real question from your pain point data
- Product positioned as "the research made actionable" throughout
- End CTA block uses Cardone directness: "Stop learning the wrong skills"

**AEO optimization (getting cited by LLMs):**
- H2 headers written as questions LLMs would answer
- Answer the question in the first sentence under each H2
- Include the research data with specific numbers
- Author bio on every page with LinkedIn URL
- Clear topic focus — one pain point per article, not multiple

**What gets site penalized by Google:**
- Thin content under 500 words
- Keyword stuffing
- Hidden text or links
- Duplicate content copied from other sites
- Pages with no original value (pure AI slop with no human insight)

### 4. Email Newsletter (Kit)

**Link rule:** Full freedom — you own the list. But overloading with
links reduces click-through rates. Maximum 2-3 links per email.

**CTA placement:**
- One primary CTA per email — don't split attention
- Place after the key value section, not at the top
- P.S. line for secondary CTA — highest read rate after subject line

**Link placement:**
- Primary CTA: hyperlinked button text or bold linked phrase
- P.S.: plain text link — feels personal not promotional

**CTA format:**
```
[Primary CTA — after value section]
The full breakdown is in the free guide.
→ Download "The AI Skills That Won't Be Replaced" here: [URL]

[P.S. — end of email]
P.S. If you found this useful, forward it to one IT professional
you know who's trying to figure out the AI pivot. They'll thank you.
```

**Cardone positioning in email:**
- Subject line names the pain or the cost — never generic
- Opening line continues from subject — no "hope you're well"
- One insight delivered completely — not teased
- Direct language throughout: "here's what you do" not "you might want to consider"
- Product mentioned once, positioned as logical continuation not interruption
- P.S. uses social proof ask — forwarding builds list organically

**What gets emails marked spam:**
- Spam trigger words: free money, guarantee, no risk, act now, limited time
- ALL CAPS in subject line
- Excessive exclamation marks
- Image-heavy emails with little text
- Sending to unengaged subscribers repeatedly
- No unsubscribe link (illegal under CAN-SPAM)

### 5. Quora

**Link rule:** Links allowed in answers but Quora moderators remove
answers that appear primarily promotional. Value must come first.
Maximum one link per answer, at the end, framed as additional resource.

**CTA placement:** Final sentence only — after complete valuable answer.
**Link placement:** Last line, framed as "I wrote more on this here."

**CTA format:**
```
[End of complete answer — after full value delivered]
I went deeper on this specific question in a free guide based on
analysis of 50 AI videos and 2,900 audience comments — covers all
7 skills with a 90-day roadmap: [Kit landing page URL]
```

**Cardone positioning on Quora:**
- Answer the question completely and directly — no teasing
- State your credentials in first sentence: "After analyzing 50 videos..."
- Use data to back every claim
- Be more direct than other answerers — Quora rewards specificity
- Position guide as "more detail on what I just told you" not "buy this"

**What gets answers removed:**
- Answers that are primarily promotional
- Same link posted across multiple answers in short timeframe
- Answers that don't actually answer the question
- Spam or affiliate links without disclosure
- Copy-pasted answers across multiple questions

**Safe frequency:** Maximum 3-5 answers per day. Space links — don't
put your URL in every answer. Every 3rd answer maximum.

---

### 6. Medium

**Link rule:** Links allowed but Medium suppresses distribution of
articles it considers promotional. Keep links to 1-2 per article.
Do NOT republish identical content from your website — use canonical
tags or rewrite sufficiently.

**CTA placement:** End of article only — one CTA.
**Link placement:** Final paragraph, framed as resource.

**CTA format:**
```
[End of article]
If you want the full research breakdown with the 90-day roadmap,
I put it together as a free guide: [Kit landing page URL]
Follow me here on Medium for weekly analysis — or connect on
LinkedIn: [LinkedIn URL]
```

**Canonical tag:** If republishing from your website, add canonical
tag pointing to your website URL. Prevents Google from treating
Medium version as the original.

**What gets Medium articles removed:**
- Pure marketing content with no original insight
- Clickbait headlines that don't match content
- Excessive self-promotion
- Plagiarized content

---

### 7. Facebook Groups

**Link rule:** Group-dependent — always check pinned rules before posting.
Most groups allow links in comments but not post body.
Some groups ban all external links entirely.

**CTA placement:** Post body — value only, no link.
**Link placement:** Comment on your own post — immediately after posting.

**CTA format:**
```
[Post body — value only]
[Full content without link]

What's your experience with this? Drop it below.

[Your comment — immediately after]
I put together a free guide on exactly this for IT professionals —
happy to share it if useful: [Kit landing page URL]
```

**Cardone positioning in Facebook groups:**
- Softer than LinkedIn — community tone, not broadcast tone
- Lead with a question or observation, not a statement
- Deliver the value, then invite discussion
- Product mention feels like helpful sharing, not selling
- Never lead with the product — always lead with the person's problem

**What gets posts removed:**
- External links in post body (most groups)
- Promotional content in groups that ban it
- Same post across multiple groups on same day
- Not being an active group member before posting

## The Master Content Template

Use this structure for every piece of content regardless of platform.
Adjust length and tone for each platform — keep the bones.

```
HOOK (platform-specific format)
Name the pain in the reader's exact words.
Make them think "that's exactly my situation."

AMPLIFY
State the cost of the problem continuing.
Not vague future cost — specific present cost.
"Right now, this is costing you [X]."

INSIGHT (the value delivery)
The one thing they don't know that changes how they see the problem.
Back it with your research data.
This section cannot be skipped — it earns the right to the CTA.

POSITION
Frame your product/service as the logical next step.
Not "buy this" — "this is what takes the insight and makes it actionable."
One sentence. Direct. No hedging.

CTA (platform-specific placement)
Direct instruction. Assumes they want it.
"Grab it here" not "if you're interested you might want to check out"
Link placed per platform rules above.
```

## Cardone Phrases That Work for IT Audience

Use these as templates — rewrite in your voice:

**Pain naming:**
- "Your [skill/approach] has an expiration date."
- "Right now, [X] is happening and most IT professionals don't see it."
- "The organizations hiring for AI roles are not looking for what you think."
- "Every week you spend learning [X] is a week you're not building [Y]."

**Data credibility:**
- "After analyzing 50 videos and 2,900 audience comments, here's what actually matters."
- "The data doesn't care about your feelings. Here's what it says."
- "This isn't opinion. This is what your target employers are actually asking for."

**Solution positioning:**
- "This is exactly what [product name] gives you — the research made actionable."
- "I built this because nobody else was answering this question with data."
- "You don't need another course. You need to know which skills actually pay."

**CTA language:**
- "Grab it here." (not "click here if you want")
- "Get the full breakdown." (not "learn more")
- "Download the guide." (not "access our free resource")
- "It's free. No strings." (removes friction)

**Phrases to never use:**
- "I hope this helps"
- "Just my thoughts"
- "You might want to consider"
- "Some people find that"
- "It could potentially"
- "Journey" (per standing instructions)

## Weekly Content Production Sequence

Using the 3-hour weekly budget:

**Hour 1 (Monday/Tuesday) — Create master content:**
Write the core insight for the week.
One pain point. One insight. Full depth.
This becomes the master article on your website.

**Hour 2 (same day or next day) — Atomize and engage:**
Generate 3 platform versions from the master:
- LinkedIn post (200 words)
- Email newsletter version (300 words)
- Quora answer (300 words)
Post LinkedIn. Schedule email. Queue Quora for Thursday.
Reply to all comments from previous week's post.

**Hour 3 (Thursday/Friday) — Build assets:**
Rotate weekly: paid PDF content, Email sequence additions,
website article formatting, Pinterest pin creation.

**Automated (Stage 7-8 — future):**
Content atomizer drafts all 7 versions from master.
You review folder, approve or edit each file.
Publisher posts approved versions on schedule.
