## **SECTION 1: EXECUTIVE SUMMARY**

Standard corporate marketing fails with technical infrastructure professionals. This audience has a finely tuned B.S. detector and ignores generic hooks. That part of the original strategy was correct and is preserved.

What changed is the mechanics. Dropping a raw landing-page link in the body of a feed post does cost reach, but the cost is modest (~15–20%, not 60%) and is mostly a function of whether the post earns engagement on its own. The real wins are not in dodging an imaginary penalty — they are in **routing the link to the surfaces that reward it** and **winning the first 90 minutes after each post.**

This blueprint runs on three pillars:

1. **The CTA Placement Matrix** — link-free feed bodies that route to the Featured Section, with hard links reserved for the newsletter and articles.
2. **Sensible Spacing + First-90-Minute Engagement** — one post per day, spaced out, with deliberate early-engagement work that decides how far each post travels.
3. **The Dan Kennedy Copy Model** — heavy, high-contrast, self-preservation-focused direct-response CTAs (preserved from the original; this was a strength).

### **1. Feed Posts (Text, Single Images, and PDF Carousels)**

* **What actually happens with links:** LinkedIn optimizes for on-platform dwell time, so a body link gets a *modest* reach reduction — roughly 15–20% on the median, and largely offset when the post itself earns strong engagement. It is a tax, not a wall.
* **Primary route — the Profile Funnel (default):** Keep the body 100% link-free and drive readers to your **Featured Section**, where the landing-page link is permanently pinned. Zero penalty in the post or the comments, and it works on every post forever.
* **Optional route — first comment (test, do not default):** The first-comment workaround is decaying and contested (see Section 3). If you test it: publish a complete, link-free value dump, then **add the link comment immediately**, and **never announce "link in comments" in the body** — link-intent language can itself get a post flagged. Measure comment-link clicks against Featured-Section clicks before trusting it.

### **2. LinkedIn Articles (Long-Form Native Publisher)**

* **What actually happens with links:** No meaningful penalty. Articles are native, crawlable destinations; readers stay on-platform. Link freely.
* **Execution:** A contextual hyperlinked text anchor roughly halfway through the body, plus a prominent, bolded CTA block at the very bottom for readers who finish.
* **Reality check:** Articles get far less feed distribution than posts. Use a feed post to drive people *to* the article — the article is a depth/credibility asset, not a reach engine.

### **3. LinkedIn Newsletters (Subscriber Push + Email)**

* **What actually happens with links:** No penalty. Newsletters bypass the feed and deliver to subscribers via push notification and email. This is your **strongest conversion channel** because it lands in inboxes.
* **Execution — dual placement:**
  * **Top CTA:** a high-contrast text-link CTA within the first ~15% of the body, directly after the opening hook, for high-intent readers.
  * **Bottom CTA:** a heavy, Dan Kennedy-style direct command block at the absolute bottom for fully engaged readers.

## **SECTION 3: ALGORITHMIC GROUNDING (CORRECTED, AS OF JUNE 2026)**

1. **Link reach impact (~15–20%, quality-confounded).** The largest independent study (Richard van der Blom, ~1.3M posts, 2026) found one in-body link reduced *median* reach by about **18.8%** — not 60%. A separate Q1 2026 analysis (Saywhat, ~400K posts) found link-*heavy*, resource-rich posts actually out-reached link-free posts, because the engagement they earn overrides the link drag. LinkedIn's own product leadership has publicly stated there is no intentional penalty for links when a post leads with value. **Conclusion: links cost some reach, but content quality dominates.**
2. **The first-comment workaround is decaying.** Van der Blom's 2026 data finds comments containing external links are now suppressed, with visibility reduced by **up to 80%**. Some other trackers still report a small residual edge for comment links (roughly a 5–10% hit vs. 40–50% for an in-body link). The evidence is genuinely mixed — which is exactly why the **Featured Section is the default** and comment links are a test, not a foundation.
3. **Spacing (one post/day, ~18–28 hours apart).** Posting 2+ times in a day shows a median per-post reach drop north of 40%, so don't stack. Beyond that, there is **no evidence** of a hard "cannibalization cap" or a "48-hour protected window." Space posts a day apart and you're fine.
4. **The first ~90 minutes decide reach.** Most of a post's distribution is determined within about 90 minutes of publishing, and only ~5% of posts that stall in the first hour recover. Early engagement velocity is the single biggest lever — bigger than any spacing rule. (See the Launch Window Protocol below.)
5. **Format: carousels/documents win.** PDF document posts carry the highest engagement of any format (~6.6%), so the carousel-heavy mix is correct — keep it.

**Structural caveat:** LinkedIn does not publish its ranking algorithm (the current model is an interest-graph system, "360Brew"). Everything here is third-party measurement and inference. The **directional** calls are well-supported (links cost some reach; native + email convert without penalty; early engagement decides distribution; carousels perform). Precise percentages should be treated as signal, not gospel.

## **THE LAUNCH WINDOW PROTOCOL (Run this on every posting day)**

This replaces the old "protect the post by going silent" thinking. The goal is to manufacture early engagement velocity in the first 90 minutes.

* **T-minus 15 min:** Leave thoughtful comments on 5–10 relevant posts in your niche. This warms your visibility and signals account activity before you publish.
* **T-0:** Publish.
* **0–90 min:** Stay at the keyboard. Reply to **every** comment with a real, specific response (never "thanks!"). Ask a follow-up question to extend each thread — comment threads trigger the most reach expansion.
* **Why:** Reach is largely locked in within ~90 minutes; a slow first hour rarely recovers.
