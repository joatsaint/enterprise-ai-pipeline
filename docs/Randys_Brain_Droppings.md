## 1. The Safety Guy Who Never Touched a Server
**Where:** Dow Chemical network build, Orange, Texas
**Type:** Don't-be-this-guy
**Status:** Used in the "Top AI Questions" YouTube script — Q1 ("Will AI take my job?")

I'm on a big network build at Dow Chemical. They were legally splitting off from a company they'd merged with years back, so we were standing up two completely separate physical networks just to keep the data apart — plus deploying new PCs out to the engineers.

During the build they hire a guy. Nice enough. But his actual background was *safety* — chemical-plant safety. Almost zero IT. Never touched a server, never built a desktop past the one in his own living room.

But he was taking a network security certification, and he would not stop telling everybody about the $140,000 security-ops job he was gonna land the second he finished the course.

Meanwhile, he's policing the office down to the micro-minutia. I had a pushcart by my desk, partly sticking into the aisle. He calls it a fire hazard — gets up and moves my cart himself.

I just kept my mouth shut. Had one of those movie moments where you look at a guy and think: *he's never gonna make it.* Not because he wasn't smart — because he had the certificate and none of the hands-on, he was alienating every single person he worked with, and you don't walk into a $140K security job with zero idea how a server actually falls over.

**The point:** A certification is not experience. Knowing the vocabulary of security isn't the same as knowing how the thing works when it's on fire.

## 2. The Casino Guy Who "Cleaned Up" the Driver Disks
**Where:** Casino (security guard who wanted into IT)
**Type:** Don't-be-this-guy
**Status:** Used in the "Top AI Questions" YouTube script — Q3 ("Can I trust what AI tells me?")

Cheap shop. They hire a casino security guard who wants into IT — zero real experience past his home computer. His fatal trait: he'd just *do things* without telling anyone.

We had a container of factory driver floppy disks for our Dell PCs — twenty-five, thirty of them. The only thing written on each disk was "Dell." The dividers between them were the only thing identifying which model each set went with. That stack saved us *hours* on every reimage — no hunting the internet for the exact motherboard and the exact drivers.

New guy decides he's gonna be helpful and clean things up. Throws away everything but two or three disks to keep his own backup copies. Didn't ask anybody. Just did it.

From then on, every reimage cost us an extra hour or two out on the internet figuring out exactly which drivers we needed. He didn't last long — the manager got tired of the random, unilateral moves. And remember, a casino is one of the most regulated, camera-everywhere, watched-down-to-the-square-inch-of-the-gaming-floor environments there is. The last place on earth to be freelancing.

**The point:** Acting autonomously — confidently, helpfully — without judgment or communication is how you destroy something valuable you never understood in the first place.

## 3. The Morning Safety Forms (and How I Got Out of Grunt Work)
**Where:** Dow Chemical network build, Orange, Texas
**Type:** Do-be-this-guy (the positive mirror)
**Status:** STANDALONE VIDEO — not part of the eight AI questions

A seasoned IT guy hired on with me for the Dow build. Good guy, we got along — but not the sharpest cookie in the box, and his real problem was he never learned to take initiative. He'd stand around and wait to be told what to do. In this profession, that's a career-path killer.

Here's the pattern that proved it. Every single morning, our foreman drove 90 minutes from Houston down to Orange, sat down, and filled out two to four safety forms — describing the work, the method, and the hazard controls. A solid 30 to 45 minutes, every morning, before anybody could move. And then we'd *also* have to wait around for the safety guy to be free to sign off before we could start.

Dead time. So after a couple weeks of watching this, I figured — I'm here before the foreman gets in anyway. Why don't I just fill the forms out for him? We barely knew each other, so this was a deliberate trust-builder.

My reasoning, three parts:
1. We get on the job faster.
2. He learns he can count on me — that he doesn't have to stand over me and bird-dog every move.
3. The honest, slightly selfish part: once he trusts me with the small stuff, he hands me bigger and bigger stuff, and I climb out of hauling PCs and racking servers into higher-level work he doesn't have time for. And on top of that — he stops watching me all the time.

And that's exactly how it played out. He started handing me the jobs he didn't want to do himself: meeting the vendors and contractors at the main gate, driving them to their work sites, making sure their paperwork was complete before they started.

So while my partner was still pulling cable and assembling PCs in the heat, I was driving the truck around in the A/C, opening doors, watching what every vendor did, learning their skill sets, and quietly making network connections — and future job contacts — all on the company dime.

**The point:** Initiative *is* judgment in motion. Don't wait to be told. Spot the friction, fix it before you're asked, and earn the trust that gets you off the grunt work and into the work that actually compounds.
