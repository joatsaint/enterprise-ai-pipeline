# This Is 1999 for AI — And You're Already in Position
**Why the IT Professionals Who Move First Will Own the Next Decade**

---

In 1999, I watched a colleague go from "the computer guy" to "you need to be in this meeting" in about six months.

He had not gotten smarter. He had not gone back to school. He had not attended a single conference about the future of enterprise technology.

What had changed was this: his company had finally decided they needed IT, and he was already standing at the door when they knocked.

---

That was the thing about the dot-com era nobody talks about now.

Companies did not know precisely what they needed IT for. They just knew they needed it. Email infrastructure. Web presence. Networks that could scale. Y2K remediation. An environment that would not collapse under the weight of the business growing faster than anyone had planned.

None of those executives had a specific job description ready. What they had was a general urgency and a budget to address it.

The people who got the call first were not the ones with the best credentials. They were the ones already doing the work. The ones who understood the systems. The ones who had been the "computer person" in the building long before the building cared about having one.

Those people did not just keep their jobs.

They got promoted. They got budget. They got headcount. Some of them got titles that lasted the next twenty years. They became IT Directors, infrastructure leads, the people senior leadership still calls when something important breaks.

The ones who waited — who figured they would figure out their positioning once the dust settled — watched someone else step into the role.

---

## Why Is AI Creating the Same Opportunity That IT Had in 1999?

The same dynamic is running right now. Different technology. Same window.

Companies do not know what they need AI for. They just know they need it. Process automation. Document handling. Customer service workflows. Decision support. Somewhere in the budget cycle, a conversation is happening about AI that started with a board presentation nobody fully understood.

The urgency is real. The clarity is not.

And at some point — maybe this year, maybe next year, depending on how fast your organization moves — someone in senior leadership is going to walk into a meeting and say something like this:

*"We need to start doing something with AI. Does anyone here actually know anything about this?"*

Two things happen in that moment.

In the first version, nobody raises their hand. The company hires a consultant from outside. Someone who understands the model but has never seen your change management process, never approved a workflow near production, never answered a 2 AM page when an automation made a decision it should not have made. That person becomes the internal AI lead. You spend the next three years explaining your environment to them.

In the second version, you raise your hand. Not because you became an AI engineer. Because you spent the previous several months learning enough to be the credible first answer in that room.

One of those outcomes is still available to you right now.

---

## Why Does an Experienced IT Operator Have an Advantage in This AI Transition?

The advantage is not technical fluency with AI models. The advantage is that you already know the environment those models will be deployed into.

**The prompt engineer knows what the AI can do. You know what it is allowed to do. Those are not the same conversation.**

Every enterprise AI deployment eventually runs into the same questions. What data can the AI see? Who approves the workflow before it touches anything important? Can it reach production? Where is the audit trail? Who owns the outcome at 2 AM when the automation runs and something unexpected happens?

A consultant hired from outside cannot answer those questions about your specific organization. You can. You have been answering variations of those questions for years, about different systems, in different contexts. This is not a new skill. It is an existing skill applied to a new problem.

The full-stack developer boom rewarded people who could connect applications. The AI boom is going to reward people who can connect automation to enterprise reality — permission, governance, data boundaries, production consequences, and the accountability that comes with all of it.

That is not prompt engineering. That is systems judgment. And experienced IT professionals have more of it than they typically give themselves credit for.

---

## What Jobs Is AI Actually Eliminating First?

Here is something worth paying attention to right now.

Uber just cut nearly a quarter of their HR and people division. Not infrastructure. Not operations engineers. Recruiters. Coordinators. Onboarding specialists. The people whose entire job was managing processes around technical work — not doing technical work.

The roles AI is eliminating in the first wave are process-heavy, document-heavy, coordination-heavy. Screening. Template communication. Compliance paperwork. The things that live next to technical operations, not inside them.

Your ticket queue is still full. Your change windows are still running. The blast radius is still yours to manage.

The first wave is not the one that hits IT operations. The first wave is the one that creates urgency — and once that urgency reaches leadership, they start looking internally for someone who can translate AI into their specific environment.

That is the moment. And right now, in most organizations, nobody is standing in that position yet.

---

## What Does Being the First Mover in AI Actually Require?

Not a certification. Not a six-month course. Not a career change.

Pick one workflow. Something low-stakes. Something where the AI assists and a human approves every step before anything consequential happens. Summarize incoming tickets. Draft template responses from existing documentation. Convert scattered troubleshooting notes into a clean SOP. Organize the communication that goes out before a maintenance window.

Run it. Document what the AI can do. Define what it cannot touch. Establish the boundary between AI assistance and human approval — the line your organization can actually sign off on.

That is your proof of concept. That is what you bring to the meeting when the question gets asked.

The person who raises their hand in that moment does not need to know everything about AI. They need to know enough about their specific environment to make the answer credible and safe.

You already have twenty-five years of specific environment knowledge. You know where the bodies are buried, which systems have never been fully documented, which vendor promises have never survived contact with production. A junior consultant hired from outside does not have any of that.

You are closer to being ready than you think. The gap between where you are now and "first credible answer in the room" is one workflow and the willingness to raise your hand.

---

## What Should Experienced IT Professionals Do Right Now?

Here is the part that matters for your career.

Do not wait for your organization to create a formal AI role. By the time they do, someone else will have already filled it informally — the person who started six months earlier, ran one safe workflow, and became the default answer to every AI question before anyone knew there was a question to answer.

The window from 1999 did not stay open forever. It closed the moment the company formalized the IT Director position and posted the job listing. The people who were already doing the work walked into that interview as the obvious internal candidate. The ones who had been watching from the sidelines interviewed as a long shot.

Pick the workflow. Define the boundary. Run the pilot. Document what worked and what the AI was not allowed to touch.

Then be in the room when the question gets asked.

**The next decade in enterprise IT belongs to the people who decided to be early. Not the fastest. Not the smartest. The ones who moved before the urgency arrived and everybody else started moving at the same time.**

That is how 1999 worked. A good sysadmin who moved first became the IT Director. A good sysadmin who waited became someone who reported to the IT Director.

Same dynamic. New technology. The window is open right now.
