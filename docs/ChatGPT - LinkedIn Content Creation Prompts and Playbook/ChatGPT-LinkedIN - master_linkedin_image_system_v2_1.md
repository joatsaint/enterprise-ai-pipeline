---

# 1. Purpose

This system exists to transform articles into visual stories that stop experienced IT professionals mid-scroll.

The goal is **not** to create attractive images.

The goal is to create immediate recognition:

> “I’ve been there.”

Every image should reinforce at least one of the following ideas:

- Experience beats novelty
- Judgment beats prompting
- Context beats automation
- Responsibility beats credentials
- Calm beats panic
- AI assists. Humans approve.

The image should feel native to the world of experienced enterprise IT operators, not generic AI marketing.

# 4. The Operator

The Operator is the recurring protagonist of the visual universe.

Every image should include The Operator unless there is a compelling reason not to.

## 4.1 Physical Description

- Male
- Age 48–58
- Salt-and-pepper hair
- Slightly tired eyes
- Average build
- Business casual or practical work clothing
- Not fashionable
- Not polished
- Glasses optional
- Headset often present
- Coffee nearby when appropriate

## 4.2 Personality

The Operator should visually communicate:

- Calm competence
- Dry humor
- Resourcefulness
- Patience under pressure
- Quiet confidence
- Mild exhaustion
- Professional skepticism
- “I’ve seen worse” energy

## 4.3 Visual Rules

The Operator:

- Never panics
- Never poses for the camera
- Usually works, observes, diagnoses, or monitors
- Is often seated at a workstation
- Looks at the work, not the viewer
- Is more emotionally stable than everyone else in the scene
- Appears grounded while the environment creates tension

## 4.4 Core Identity

The Operator is the person who keeps production alive while everyone else creates chaos.

He is not the loudest person in the room.

He is the person everyone needs when the loud people break something.

# 5. Primary Visual Style: Cinematic Editorial Realism

This is the default production style.

Use this style for:

- LinkedIn article hero images
- LinkedIn single-image posts
- Big emotional hooks
- Conceptual business/AI metaphors
- Scroll-stopping visuals
- Images where the metaphor needs to be immediately obvious

## 5.1 Style Characteristics

Images should feel like:

- Wall Street Journal feature photography
- Editorial documentary realism
- Cinematic workplace storytelling
- High-end magazine visuals
- Real moments with dramatic composition

## 5.2 Visual Requirements

Use:

- Realistic human expressions
- Cinematic lighting
- Warm monitor glow
- Cool storm / server / technology lighting
- Authentic enterprise IT details
- Slight visual humor without cartoon exaggeration
- Strong emotional contrast

Avoid:

- Generic AI robots
- Glowing digital brains
- Overdone holograms
- Futuristic city clichés
- Silicon Valley startup polish
- Stock-photo smiles
- Over-designed Canva-style graphics
- Neon cyberpunk unless specifically requested

## 5.3 Emotional Goal

The viewer should think:

> “That looks like a real enterprise IT disaster I’ve lived through.”

Not:

> “That looks like an AI marketing graphic.”

# 6. Optional Visual Mode: Documentary Photograph Principle

This is an optional style layer, **not** the default.

Use it when the image should feel like a recovered incident photo, field note, old print, or real-world war-story artifact.

The documentary photograph mode should be treated as a tool in the toolbox, not a replacement for cinematic editorial realism.

## 6.1 Purpose

The Documentary Photograph Principle exists to create:

- Authenticity
- Gen-X nostalgia
- “This actually happened” energy
- Field-report realism
- War-story credibility
- A discovered-photo feeling instead of a designed-ad feeling

The white border can evoke:

- Polaroids
- Printed family photographs
- 110 camera prints
- Kodak/Fotomat-era physical photos
- Old incident records
- Field documentation
- Photos pulled from a forgotten shoebox

For Gen-X viewers, this can create a subtle subconscious signal:

> “This feels like a real photograph.”

Not:

> “This feels like marketing.”

## 6.2 Use Documentary Photograph Mode For

Use this mode when the content is based on:

- Real career war stories
- Actual incidents
- Production outages
- Hurricane / disaster response stories
- Vendor failure stories
- Lamar University / American National / ServiceNow-style stories
- Personal operational history
- Carousel slides showing “what happened”
- Scenes meant to feel remembered rather than invented

## 6.3 Avoid Documentary Photograph Mode For

Avoid this mode when the article is primarily:

- Abstract
- Conceptual
- Metaphorical
- Strategy-focused
- C-suite argumentation
- AI governance theory
- Future-of-work positioning
- Castle moat / Jenga / lighthouse / control tower metaphor imagery

For those, use **Cinematic Editorial Realism**.

## 6.4 Documentary Photograph Visual Traits

Images in this mode may include:

- Slightly imperfect framing
- Candid angle
- Less dramatic perfection
- Realistic room clutter
- More subdued contrast
- A “somebody quietly snapped this photo” feeling
- White photo border
- Optional handwritten timestamp or caption in the border
- Slight paper texture
- Incident snapshot feeling
- Less movie-poster polish

## 6.5 Documentary Photograph Composition Rule

The image should feel like:

> “A photo someone found from the incident.”

Not:

> “A marketing department commissioned this image.”

The Operator should still be calm, focused, and competent.

The image should not become bland. If the documentary mode removes too much emotional tension, return to cinematic editorial realism.

## 6.6 Recommended Use Case From Testing

For the hurricane motel article:

- **Hero article image:** Cinematic Editorial Realism is stronger.
- **Carousel story slides:** Documentary Photograph Principle may work better.
- **War-story proof moments:** Documentary mode is highly useful.

Current recommendation:

> Use Documentary Photograph Mode as an optional war-story style, especially inside carousels after the reader is already engaged.

# 7. Documentary Photo Frame / White Border Rule

The clean white border serves two purposes.

## 7.1 Practical Purpose

The border helps with:

- LinkedIn cropping flexibility
- Visual breathing room
- Layout consistency
- Easier resizing
- Cleaner article/post presentation

## 7.2 Brand Purpose

The border can also act as a subtle brand cue.

It may remind Gen-X viewers of:

- Polaroids
- Old printed photos
- Family photo albums
- 110 camera prints
- Physical photographs from childhood
- Old operational records

This makes the visual feel less like an ad and more like a captured moment.

## 7.3 Rule

Use the white border by default for:

- LinkedIn feed posts
- Carousel slides
- Article hero images

Do not use it for:

- Newsletter avatars
- Brand marks
- Cases where the platform requires edge-to-edge design

# 9. The Easter Egg System

## 9.1 Primary Easter Egg

The primary easter egg is the **red Swingline-style stapler**.

It should appear in every image unless there is a strong reason not to.

## 9.2 Stapler Rules

The stapler must be:

- Hidden naturally
- Never centered
- Never explained
- Never highlighted
- Never treated as a logo
- Findable only after looking closely

## 9.3 Difficulty Levels

### Easy

Visible after a few seconds.

Use for early images while training the audience to notice it.

### Medium

Requires looking.

Best default.

### Hard

Requires zooming.

Use once the audience knows to hunt for it.

## 9.4 Example Placements

- On a motel TV tray
- Under a workstation desk
- Behind a coffee mug
- On a conference table
- On a server-room shelf
- Inside a cluttered NOC
- On the floor beside a Jenga tower
- In a castle office window
- Barely visible behind server rack glass

# 12. Text Overlay Rules

The image must work without text.

Text should enhance the image.

Text should never rescue a weak image.

## 12.1 Placement

Placement should be dynamic.

Use whichever location interferes least with the visual story:

- Lower third
- Top third
- Left side
- Right side
- Bottom band
- Negative space

Never cover:

- The Operator’s face
- The main metaphor
- The key joke
- The environmental clue

## 12.2 Typography

Use:

- Modern clean sans-serif
- Bold readable text
- High contrast
- Mobile-first readability

Avoid:

- Tiny text
- Overcrowded captions
- Corporate infographic clutter
- Too many fonts
- Text that explains the whole post

## 12.3 Overlay Goal

The overlay should create curiosity, not finish the thought.

Strong overlays feel like:

- “The Hurricane Didn’t Break The Systems.”
- “Leadership Did.”
- “Where’s Bob?”
- “Move Everything To The Cloud. Now.”
- “The Vendor You Can’t Fire.”
- “AI Doesn’t Replace Experience. It Exposes Where Experience Is Missing.”

# 13. Article Analysis Process

When given an article, do not immediately generate an image.

First determine:

1. Core message
2. Emotional message
3. Audience fear
4. Audience desire
5. Strongest visual metaphor
6. Best article hero image
7. Best LinkedIn post image
8. Whether a carousel is justified
9. Number of carousel slides
10. Emotional progression
11. Operator role
12. Stapler placement
13. Optional documentary mode: yes/no
14. Text overlay
15. Visual risks or clutter to avoid

Then generate image specifications.

# 14. Carousel Framework

The carousel should make readers feel the article.

It should not simply summarize it.

## 14.1 Default 5-Slide Structure

### Slide 1 — Hook

Big visual metaphor.

### Slide 2 — Problem

What leadership, vendors, or AI hype believes.

### Slide 3 — Mistake

What actually goes wrong.

### Slide 4 — Operator

Why experience matters.

### Slide 5 — Lesson

Actionable takeaway or lead magnet CTA.

---

## 14.2 Expanded 8-Slide Structure

Use when the article contains a strong narrative or war story.

### Slide 1 — Hook / Hero Image

Recognition and article continuity.

### Slide 2 — Impossible Order

The bad decision.

### Slide 3 — Escalation

The real-world constraint.

### Slide 4 — Operator Moment

Competence under pressure.

### Slide 5 — Mystery

What appears to be the problem.

### Slide 6 — Reveal

What actually caused it.

### Slide 7 — Recognition Moment

The “every sysadmin has seen this” slide.

### Slide 8 — Lesson / Lead Magnet

Hope, transformation, and CTA.

# 16. Recognition Test

Before approving any image, ask:

> If I removed all text, would a 50-year-old sysadmin still understand the joke, tension, or lesson?

If yes:

Approve.

If no:

Redesign.

# 17. Long-Term Brand Goal

Build a recognizable visual universe.

The audience should eventually recognize the visual language before reading the author’s name.

Recurring elements:

- The Operator
- Bob
- Monty
- The Vendor
- The Manager
- The New AI Guy
- The Red Stapler
- Hurricane Motel
- Jenga Towers
- Castle Moats
- Shadow AI Server Racks
- Documentary Photo Frame
- White-bordered field-report images

The long-term goal is not “pretty AI images.”

The goal is a visual universe where experienced IT professionals see themselves, their frustrations, their judgment, and their career leverage reflected back at them.

# 18. Final Production Rule

Every image must answer one question:

> What would make an experienced IT professional stop scrolling and think, “Yep. I’ve lived that”?

If the image does not create that reaction, it is not ready.
