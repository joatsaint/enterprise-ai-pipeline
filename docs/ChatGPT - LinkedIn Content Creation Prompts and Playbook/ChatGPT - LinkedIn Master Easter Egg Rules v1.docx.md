# Easter Egg Rules v1

## Sysadmin AI Survival Playbook / IT→AI Transition Playbook

Version: 1.0  
Status: Active Visual Brand Reference  
Purpose: Define the recurring easter egg system for LinkedIn visuals, with the Red Swingline-style stapler as the primary hidden object.

---

# 1\. Purpose

The Easter Egg System exists to create recognition, repeat-viewing behavior, and subtle audience participation without cheap engagement bait.

The goal is not to hide random objects.

The goal is to build a visual habit.

Over time, repeat readers should start looking for familiar objects before they even read the full post.

The easter eggs should support the larger brand universe:

* The Operator

* enterprise IT chaos

* Gen-X recognition

* dry workplace humor

* operational memory

* “I’ve been there” energy

* AI assists. Humans approve.

---

# 2\. Primary Easter Egg

## The Red Swingline-Style Stapler

The red stapler is the primary easter egg.

It should appear in every major image unless there is a strong reason not to.

It represents:

* ignored expertise

* corporate absurdity

* Office Space energy

* practical tools overlooked by shiny trends

* the experienced operator nobody notices until something breaks

* the boring object that becomes important at the worst possible moment

The stapler is not flashy.

That is the point.

It is an ordinary office object that becomes a recurring symbol of operational reality.

---

# 3\. Core Stapler Rules

The red stapler must follow these rules:

1. Never center it.

2. Never spotlight it.

3. Never explain it in the post.

4. Never label it.

5. Never make it the main subject.

6. Never make it look like a logo.

7. Never turn it into a mascot.

8. Never use it as obvious engagement bait.

9. Always make it feel naturally placed.

10. Make it visible enough to reward close looking.

The correct reaction is not:

“Why is there a stapler?”

The correct reaction is:

“Wait. Is that the stapler again?”

---

# 4\. Why The Stapler Works

The stapler works because it is not an AI object.

It is not futuristic.

It is not impressive.

It is not part of the official story.

That is exactly why it fits.

Most AI content shows:

* robots

* glowing brains

* holograms

* futuristic cities

* neon dashboards

This brand uses:

* coffee

* old runbooks

* server racks

* whiteboards

* ticket queues

* motel workstations

* Jenga towers

* bad dashboards

* forgotten infrastructure

* one red stapler

The stapler belongs to the world of cubicles, reorgs, office absurdity, and ignored competence.

That is the audience’s world.

# 5\. Stapler Difficulty Levels

Use three levels of difficulty.

Do not use the same difficulty every time.

Variation makes the search feel alive.

---

## Level 1 — Easy

### Definition

The stapler is visible within 2–4 seconds of looking.

### Use For

* early brand training

* first few posts in a series

* busy images where too much hiding would make it impossible

* carousel covers

* images where the audience is still learning to look for it

### Examples

* on a conference table

* beside a keyboard

* near a coffee mug

* on a motel nightstand

* on a desk corner

* next to a runbook

### Risk

Too easy can become obvious.

If it feels like a prop, hide it better.

---

## Level 2 — Medium

### Definition

The stapler is visible after intentional looking.

The viewer may need to scan the image.

### Use For

* most standard images

* established audience familiarity

* LinkedIn single-image posts

* article hero images

* carousel slides after slide one

### Examples

* half-hidden behind a coffee mug

* under a TV tray

* partly covered by papers

* near a monitor base

* on a shelf in the background

* behind a laptop bag

* on the floor near cables

### Best Default

Medium should be the standard difficulty level.

It rewards attention without frustrating people.

---

## Level 3 — Hard

### Definition

The stapler requires zooming or careful inspection.

### Use For

* later-stage brand familiarity

* highly detailed images

* carousel slides where repeat readers are already engaged

* special “inside joke” visuals

* images designed to reward second looks

### Examples

* reflected in server rack glass

* tiny object on a castle office desk

* partly visible under fallen Jenga blocks

* on a shelf deep in a server room

* barely visible through a conference room doorway

* hidden near a cable tray

* peeking out of a laptop bag

### Risk

Hard should not mean invisible.

If nobody can find it, it stops being rewarding.

# 8\. Easter Egg Density Rules

Too many easter eggs weaken the image.

Use this density system:

## Standard Image

Use:

* 1 primary easter egg: red stapler

* 0–1 secondary easter egg

## Rich Carousel Slide

Use:

* 1 primary easter egg

* 1–2 secondary easter eggs

## Highly Detailed “Find The Details” Image

Use:

* 1 primary easter egg

* 2–3 secondary easter eggs maximum

## Never Do This

Do not cram every easter egg into one image.

That turns recognition into clutter.

The viewer should feel rewarded, not attacked by props.

---

# 9\. Visibility Rules

The easter egg should be findable.

The easter egg should not be obvious.

The easter egg should never distract from the main story.

Use this simple test:

## First Look

The viewer sees the main story.

## Second Look

The viewer notices details.

## Third Look

The viewer finds the stapler.

That is the ideal sequence.

If the viewer sees the stapler before the story, it is too obvious.

If the viewer cannot find the stapler after zooming, it is too hidden.

---

# 10\. What Not To Do

Do not:

* mention the stapler in the post

* circle the stapler

* point arrows at the stapler

* use “Can you find the stapler?” in every caption

* make the stapler the main subject

* turn the stapler into a logo

* put the stapler in The Operator’s hand repeatedly

* place the stapler in impossible locations

* make it glow

* make it huge

* make it too clean or polished

* use multiple red staplers in one image

* let it overpower the metaphor

The stapler is a silent observer.

Not the star.

---

# 11\. How To Use The Stapler For Engagement

The stapler should create comments naturally.

Do not beg for engagement.

Good audience behavior over time:

* “Found the stapler.”

* “Stapler spotted.”

* “Okay, that one was harder.”

* “I looked for the stapler before reading.”

* “Please tell me the stapler is intentional.”

* “The stapler universe continues.”

Let the audience discover the pattern.

When people comment, respond lightly.

Possible replies:

* “You have production-grade eyesight.”

* “Change control has acknowledged your discovery.”

* “The stapler remains in scope.”

* “That was a Level 2 placement.”

* “Operational awareness confirmed.”

* “The stapler survives another outage.”

Avoid:

* long explanations

* overbranding

* asking everyone else to find it

* turning every reply into a sales pitch

The joke works because it is understated.

---

# 12\. Caption Rules

## Default Rule

Do not mention the stapler in the post caption.

## Acceptable Rare Exceptions

After the pattern is well established, occasional subtle references may work.

Examples:

* “As always, the important details are in the room.”

* “Some objects survive every outage.”

* “The usual office artifact made it through change control.”

* “Sharp eyes may notice one recurring stakeholder.”

Use these rarely.

Never explain the whole bit.

---

# 13\. Carousel Rules

In carousels, the stapler can move from slide to slide.

It should not be in the same position every time.

## Suggested Progression

### Slide 1

Easy or medium placement.

The audience should begin the hunt.

### Slide 2–3

Medium placement.

Keep it natural.

### Slide 4–6

Medium or hard placement.

Reward closer viewing.

### Slide 7

Hard placement if the slide is visually rich.

### Slide 8

Easy placement if it is a lead magnet or lesson slide.

End with recognition, not frustration.

# 16\. Prompt Language

Use this in image prompts:

Include a small red Swingline-style stapler hidden naturally somewhere in the scene as a recurring easter egg. It should not be centered, highlighted, labeled, or explained. It should be visible only after closer inspection and should feel like an ordinary object that belongs in the environment.

## Short Version

Hide a small red Swingline-style stapler naturally in the scene, off-center, unmentioned, and visible only after close inspection.

## Hard Mode Version

Hide a small red Swingline-style stapler deep in the background, partially obscured by realistic office or IT equipment, findable only by careful inspection, never highlighted.

---

# 17\. Quality Control Checklist

Before approving an image, ask:

## Stapler Check

* Is the stapler present?

* Is it red?

* Is it off-center?

* Is it naturally placed?

* Is it visible but not obvious?

* Is it not stealing attention?

* Is there only one stapler?

## Story Check

* Does the main image still work before the stapler is noticed?

* Does the stapler reward a second look?

* Does it support the brand tone?

* Does it feel like Office Space energy without being a cheap reference?

## Clutter Check

* Are there too many easter eggs?

* Is the scene still readable on mobile?

* Does the image still pass the two-second recognition test?

* Would a 50-year-old sysadmin understand the visual joke?

# 19\. Easter Egg Philosophy

The easter eggs are not decorations.

They are proof that the visual world has continuity.

The audience should feel like each image belongs to the same operational universe.

The Red Stapler is the constant.

The Operator is the human anchor.

The metaphors are the story engine.

The secondary easter eggs are the reward for people who look closely.

Together, they create visual memory.

That memory compounds.

---

# 20\. Final Rule

The stapler is never the point.

The stapler is how people know they are still inside the same world.

Use it quietly.

Use it consistently.

Let the audience find it.

That is how the joke becomes theirs.
