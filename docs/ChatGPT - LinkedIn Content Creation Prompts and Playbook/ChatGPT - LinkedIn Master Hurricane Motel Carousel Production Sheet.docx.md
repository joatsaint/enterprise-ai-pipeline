# Hurricane Motel Carousel Production Sheet v1

## Article: “I Ran Enterprise IT From a Motel That Banned Prostitution — And It Taught Me Everything About AI”

Version: 1.0  
Status: Production Reference  
Content System: Sysadmin AI Survival Playbook / IT→AI Transition Playbook  
Primary Audience: Experienced Gen-X sysadmins, infrastructure operators, enterprise software admins, and IT professionals with 20–30 years of operational experience.

---

# 1\. Purpose

This production sheet defines the full 8-slide LinkedIn carousel for the hurricane motel / enterprise IT story.

The carousel should not merely summarize the article.

It should make the reader feel the article.

The emotional job is to create immediate recognition:

“I’ve been there.”

The story is not really about hurricanes.

It is about what happens when leadership creates chaos, systems expose hidden dependencies, and the experienced operator becomes the only thing still functioning.

---

# 2\. Core Article Message

The core message:

**AI will not replace experienced IT professionals. It will expose where experienced IT judgment is missing.**

The hurricane did not break the systems.

Bad planning, hidden dependencies, missing institutional knowledge, and leadership decisions did.

This maps directly to AI adoption:

* AI does not remove operational risk.

* AI accelerates whatever process already exists.

* AI cannot understand undocumented dependencies by magic.

* AI cannot replace the person who knows what is safe to touch.

* AI assists. Humans approve.

# 4\. Visual Rules For Entire Carousel

## Required Elements

Every slide should include:

* The established Sysadmin AI Survival Playbook visual style

* Gen-X enterprise IT realism

* no generic AI robots

* no glowing AI brains

* no startup stock-photo nonsense

* hidden Red Swingline-style stapler

* readable mobile-first overlay text

* visual meaning that still works if the text is removed

## The Operator

The Operator should appear in most slides.

He should be:

* male, early-to-mid 50s

* salt-and-pepper hair

* slightly tired but sharp-eyed

* calm under pressure

* practical clothing

* never panicking

* more emotionally stable than everyone else

* focused on production reality, not executive theater

## Primary Easter Egg

A small red Swingline-style stapler must appear in each slide.

It should be:

* off-center

* unmentioned

* naturally placed

* visible only after closer inspection

* never the main subject

## Style Mode Rule

Use **Cinematic Editorial Realism** for:

* Slide 1

* Slide 2

* Slide 5

* Slide 6

* Slide 7

* Slide 8

Use **Documentary Photograph Principle** for:

* Slide 3

* Slide 4

Reason:

Slides 3 and 4 are “this actually happened” field-report scenes. They benefit from a found-photo, incident-snapshot feeling.

# 10\. Quality Control Checklist

Before approving the carousel, ask:

## Story Arc

* Does the carousel create curiosity on Slide 1?

* Does Slide 2 show the bad decision clearly?

* Does Slide 3 show real-world stress?

* Does Slide 4 show competence under bad conditions?

* Does Slide 5 create a mystery?

* Does Slide 6 deliver the reveal?

* Does Slide 7 create recognition?

* Does Slide 8 create hope and action?

## Operator Consistency

* Does The Operator look like the same kind of person across slides?

* Is he calm under pressure?

* Does he avoid generic “AI guy” energy?

* Is he practical, grounded, and believable?

* Is he more emotionally stable than the environment?

## Easter Egg Check

* Is the red stapler present in every slide?

* Is it off-center?

* Is it naturally placed?

* Is it findable but not obvious?

* Is there only one stapler per slide?

## Visual Quality

* Does every slide work on mobile?

* Is the text readable in under two seconds?

* Does each image still communicate without text?

* Is there too much clutter?

* Does the image feel LinkedIn-native but not generic?

## Brand Check

* No robots.

* No glowing AI brains.

* No cheesy holograms.

* No stock-photo smiles.

* No Silicon Valley startup aesthetic.

* The image should feel like experienced IT reality, not AI-bro marketing.
